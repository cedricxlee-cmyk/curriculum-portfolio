Who Sees What You Share?

Unit Rationale

Online-Native Design Principle

These students are already living the lesson

Most K–2 digital citizenship curricula assume a classroom where the teacher mediates digital exposure.

This unit is designed around that reality. The home learning environment is the primary data source. Students observe their own real digital behaviors, reflect on them with a family learning coach, and build a personal Digital Citizenship Journal over four weeks. The family coach is a designed instructional partner — not just supervision — with structured conversation protocols that scaffold their role session by session.

Assessment is portfolio-based and grounded in observable behavior in the context where digital citizenship actually matters: the student's real screen, inbox, and video calls — not a simulated scenario.

The unit addresses a gap that most early childhood digital literacy programs miss: the difference between learning about digital citizenship and practicing it in a live digital environment. Because Visions students are independent study learners whose entire school day is mediated through devices, every session can connect directly to something they encountered that week. That authenticity is the pedagogical advantage.

Essential Questions & Enduring Understandings

Essential Questions
  • What does it mean to share something online?
  • How is talking online different from talking in person?
  • What information is okay to share — and with whom?
  • How do we treat people kindly when we can't see them?
  • What is a digital footprint, and does mine matter?
Enduring Understandings
  • Sharing online is permanent in ways sharing in person isn't.
  • Kindness doesn't change when we're using a screen.
  • Some information belongs to us — we get to decide who sees it.
  • Our digital actions build a picture of who we are over time.
  • The internet has a very good memory, even when we forget.

Full Lesson Plan: Week 1, Session 1

Device Inventory: What do we use, and why?

Week 1, Session 1 25 minutes Async + Coach
Learning Objectives

Students will identify at least 3 digital tools used in their home. Students will distinguish between tools used for learning, playing, and communicating. Family coach will begin using the observation protocol introduced in the Coach Guide.

0–5min
Activate
Warm-Up: Device Walk
Before any screens come on, student and coach do a physical walk around the home together and count every screen or device they can find — tablets, phones, computers, smart TVs, game controllers, smart speakers. Student tallies on a paper counting sheet (provided in Student Materials below).
Coach note: This is intentionally physical and pre-screen. It builds observation skills and sets up the conversation about "we have a lot of technology — let's think carefully about it."
5–15min
Explore
The Device Map Activity
Student opens the Device Map template (Google Slides or printable). The template has three columns: Learn, Play, Talk. Student draws or dictates the name of each device they found and places it in the column that fits best. Coach helps with spelling and prompts thinking with: "What do we use the Chromebook mostly for? Is that learning or playing or both?" Devices can go in more than one column.
Coach note: Don't correct "wrong" answers. If a student puts YouTube in "Learn," explore it with curiosity: "What do you learn from YouTube? What do you watch for fun?" The goal is reflective thinking, not right answers. Record what the student says in the Coach Observation Log.
15–22min
Discuss
Coach Conversation: The Big Question
Using the Coach Prompt Card (Session 1), coach leads a short conversation: "Of all the devices we found today — which one do you use the most? ... Why? ... What would happen if we didn't have it for a week?" Coach listens and responds naturally, then writes one observation in the log: something the student said that surprised them, or that showed how the student thinks about technology.
22–25min
Reflect
First Journal Entry
Student adds their completed Device Map to their Digital Citizenship Journal (a dedicated folder in Seesaw or a physical binder). Prompt for the journal entry: "Draw or write about your favorite thing to do online. Who is there with you?" TK/Kindergarten students may draw and dictate. Grade 1–2 students write 1–2 sentences with drawing.

Full Lesson Plan: Week 2, Session 2 ★

The "Share or Don't Share?" Sort

Week 2, Session 2 28 minutes Async + Coach
Learning Objectives

Students will categorize personal information as private, public, or "ask a grown-up first." Students will articulate a reason for at least one categorization decision. Family coach will practice using the structured observation protocol and record student reasoning verbatim for at least two items.

0–6min
Activate
Story Warm-Up: "Zara's Mistake"
Student watches or listens to a short 4-minute animated story (teacher-produced video or Common Sense Media's "Pause & Think Online") about a child who shares her home address with an online game account. The video pauses at two decision points with on-screen questions: "What should Zara do next?" Student responds aloud to coach — no writing yet.
Coach note: This is prediction, not evaluation. There's no single right answer for the pause moment. The goal is to activate thinking before the sorting activity.
6–20min
Explore
The Sort Activity
Using the digital Google Slides sort (drag-and-drop) or printed and cut paper cards, student sorts 12 information cards into three labeled bins: Safe to Share (green), Keep Private (red), and Ask a Grown-Up First (yellow). After each placement, coach asks: "Why did you put that one there?" Coach writes the student's reasoning for at least 2 items in the Observation Log — verbatim if possible. See Student Materials section below for the full card set.
Coach note: Some cards are genuinely ambiguous ("my school name," "my birthday month"). That's intentional. If a student hesitates, say "That's a tricky one — what makes it hard to decide?" Don't redirect to a correct bin.
20–26min
Discuss
Coach Debrief: Two Questions
Coach uses the Session 2 Prompt Card for a brief structured conversation:

1. "Was any card hard to decide? Which one? Why?"
2. "If a stranger online asked you for [your phone number / your address], what would you do?"

Coach listens fully before responding. If student says they'd share, respond with curiosity: "What makes that feel okay?" — not alarm. Record the student's responses.
26–28min
Reflect
Journal Entry
Student adds completed sort (screenshot of slides or photo of paper sort) to their journal. Prompt: "What is one thing you will keep private? Draw it or write it." Coach writes one sentence in the Coach section of the journal about what the student did or said that showed growth or understanding today.

Full Lesson Plan: Week 3, Session 2

Scenario Role-Play: What Would You Do?

Week 3, Session 2 30 minutes Async + Recorded Video
Learning Objectives

Students will identify unkind behavior in online scenarios and generate at least one kind alternative response. Students will record a short video explanation of their reasoning. This video becomes a performance assessment artifact.

0–5min
Activate
The Missing Voice
Coach reads a short text message exchange aloud (printed or on screen) in a flat, robotic voice — no emotion, no facial expression. Then reads it again with exaggerated emotion. Student discusses: "Which reading felt different? Why? What does that tell us about text messages?" This activates the core concept: text removes tone, expression, and body language.
5–22min
Explore
Three Scenarios — Think, Discuss, Respond
Student reads (or coach reads aloud) three short scenarios from the Scenario Cards (see Student Materials). For each scenario:

1. Student decides: Is this kind, unkind, or somewhere in between?
2. Student says what they would do if they were the person receiving the message.
3. Student comes up with a kind version of the unkind message (if applicable).

Coach does not evaluate responses — they listen, ask "why," and write notes. Students are not expected to have perfect answers.
Coach note: Scenario 3 is deliberately ambiguous (a friend doesn't respond to a message for two days). This one is meant to prompt discussion about assumptions — we often don't know why someone doesn't respond, and we sometimes assume the worst.
22–30min
Performance Task
Record Your Response Video
Student picks their favorite scenario from the three and records a 1–2 minute video (Flipgrid, Seesaw, or any video tool) explaining: "What happened in the scenario? Was it kind or unkind, and why? What would you do?" Coach helps with setup but does not script or coach the content. Video is submitted to teacher and added to the Digital Citizenship Journal portfolio.
Coach note: For TK/K students, the video can just be them acting out what they'd do. Grade 1–2 students should try to explain their reasoning. Accept whatever form of communication the student is most comfortable with — this is formative.

Teacher note on ambiguous cards: "My school name," "My birthday month," and "My city" are designed to generate discussion. There is no single correct bin for these. The goal is reasoning, not categorization accuracy. If students sort them differently, explore why — that conversation is the lesson.

Week 3: Online Kindness Scenario Cards

Role-Play Activity

Read each scenario together. Discuss before moving to the next one. All scenarios are fictional and age-appropriate for grades TK–2.

Scenario 1 — The Comment

Mia is in your class. She drew a picture of her house and posted it in your class Seesaw. You think the drawing doesn't look like a real house. You type: "That doesn't look like a house." Then you press send.

How do you think Mia feels when she reads that? Is there a kinder way to say the same thing?

Scenario 2 — The Left Out

Your class has a group chat. Four students are making plans to play an online game together after school. They don't invite Marcus. Marcus can see all the messages in the chat.

How might Marcus feel? Is leaving someone out of a plan unkind, even if you don't say anything mean?

Scenario 3 — The No Reply

You send a message to your friend Lily: "Do you want to video call tonight?" She doesn't answer. You send it again the next day. Still no answer. You start to think she's ignoring you on purpose.

What are three different reasons Lily might not have responded? Does not responding always mean someone is being unkind?

Family Learning Coach Prompt Card — Week 2, Session 2

Coach Guide

This card guides the coach conversation after the Sort activity. Use these questions in order. You don't need to ask all of them — follow the student's thinking.

During the sort, ask:

"Why did you put that one there?" (after each card)
"Is there any card you're not sure about? What makes it tricky?"

After the sort, choose 1–2 of these:

"Is there anything on that [Safe to Share] pile that surprised you?"

"If someone online you didn't know asked for your [phone number / address / real name], what would you do?"

"Can you think of a time when someone shared something that felt private — either yours, or someone else's?"

Coach observation to record:

Write one specific thing your student said or did during this activity that showed how they think about privacy. Use their exact words if you can.

Assessment System

Assessment in this unit is portfolio-based and observational. Because students are ages 4–8 and learning in home environments, traditional tests are developmentally inappropriate and ecologically invalid. The three assessment sources below give teachers a complete picture of student understanding across four weeks.

Assessment Sources
  • Digital Citizenship Journal — 4 entries (one per week), drawing/writing/dictation, submitted via Seesaw or Google Drive
  • Coach Observation Log — family coach records specific student statements each session; teacher reviews weekly
  • Performance Tasks — Week 3 video response and Week 4 Digital Footprint Portrait with narration
Teacher Review Protocol
  • Week 1–2: Review journals and Coach Logs for misconceptions; provide written feedback to coach and student via Seesaw comment
  • Week 3: View video responses; use rubric below; provide 1 warm + 1 growth comment
  • Week 4: Score Footprint Portrait using summative rubric; schedule brief 1:1 check-in if needed

Summative Rubric: Digital Footprint Portrait + Narration (Week 4)

Student creates a visual representation of the kind of digital citizen they want to be and narrates it in a 1–2 minute recording. Scored across four dimensions.

Pacing Notes for Online/Independent Study Context

Implementation Guidance

How this unit runs differently than in a traditional classroom

Session timing is flexible by design. Sessions are listed as 20–30 minutes, but the coach conversation often extends organically. That's a feature, not a problem. If a student and coach spend 45 minutes on the Sort activity because the conversation is rich, that's the best possible outcome. Teachers should communicate this permission explicitly to families in the unit launch message.

The synchronous Zoom moments are rare and purposeful. Week 3, Session 3 is the only full-class Zoom session in the unit (the Community Circle for kindness agreements). That session should not be used to introduce new content — families need to come in having already completed Sessions 1–2 of Week 3. The Zoom is for community building and celebration, not instruction.

Coach fatigue is real. Four sessions per week is ambitious. The unit is designed so that Sessions 1 and 4 of each week are lighter coach-involvement sessions (student works more independently), while Sessions 2 and 3 are the high-engagement coach sessions. Teachers should communicate this pattern to families so coaches can plan their week.

Motivation Design: The Digital Citizenship Passport

Design Rationale

The problem with reflection-only curriculum at this age

TK–2 students don't disengage the way high schoolers do — they disengage physically. The moment a session stops feeling like something they're doing and starts feeling like something they're watching or answering, you lose them. The Digital Citizenship Passport addresses this by making progress visible, tactile, and social — designed around the actual motivational structure of 4–8 year olds: pride, collection, and showing things to people they love.

The Passport is a physical or digital booklet that travels with the student across all four weeks. Each completed session unlocks a stamp — not for producing the right answer, but for doing the thing. Completing the Device Walk earns the "Explorer" stamp. Recording your first audio story earns the "Storyteller" stamp. The stamps are designed to be shared: the unit culminates with a family "Passport Celebration" where students show their completed Passports and explain one stamp they're proud of.

Why this works in the online independent study context: The family coach sees the Passport after every session. That visibility creates a social feedback loop the student cares about — not grades, not teacher approval, but their parent or guardian seeing what they did today. For this age group, that's the most powerful motivator available.

How Stamps Address the Motivation Problem
  • Progress is visible after every session — not just at the end of the unit. Students and coaches see momentum building.
  • Completion beats performance — stamps are earned by doing, not by getting things right. This removes anxiety for struggling learners.
  • The coach becomes a witness — marking the session done gives the coach a meaningful role in the student's progress narrative.
  • The Passport travels — students can show it to anyone. This is the social feedback loop that sustains motivation between sessions.
Gamification + Curriculum Integrity
  • Stamps are not awarded for correct answers — this prevents gaming and reduces anxiety
  • The coach observation log still captures quality of engagement — the stamp system handles motivation, the log handles assessment
  • The Week 4 "Full Passport" is a social milestone, not a grade — families are explicitly invited to celebrate it
  • Students who complete fewer than 8 stamps receive teacher outreach — the Passport makes support needs visible early
What Disengaged Students Can't Fake (TK-2 version)

Behavioral evidence over self-report

At this age, "faking engagement" isn't really a problem — but family coach disengagement is. A coach who marks sessions done without actually doing them is the real risk. The stamp system addresses this by requiring the coach to upload one artifact per stamp: a photo of the Device Map, a screenshot of the Sort, a Flipgrid video link. No upload, no stamp. This creates a low-burden but real accountability structure for the coach, not the student.
