Project Rationale

The Core Design Insight

The online school IS the community — build from that

The most common critique of online schooling is that it lacks community. This project inverts that critique entirely. Visions students span nine counties across Northern California. That geographic distribution isn't a limitation to work around — it's an asset no traditional school has. No brick-and-mortar school has students simultaneously embedded in Sacramento, Chico, Yuba City, Woodland, and Red Bluff. No traditional school can produce a living archive of a nine-county region built by the people who actually live there.

The Neighborhood project makes Visions' distributed enrollment the engine of the curriculum. Students don't simulate fieldwork — they do fieldwork in their actual neighborhoods, with real tools, for a real public audience. The archive they produce is genuinely novel. It could not exist without an online school's geographic reach. That's the point.

Cross-grade collaboration is structural, not supplementary. Younger students generate raw material — drawings, audio stories, family oral histories. Older students curate, contextualize, map, and publish. The relationship between grade bands mirrors how real archival and documentary work operates, where primary sources and interpretive scholarship are distinct and complementary roles. This structure gives every student a meaningful, developmentally appropriate contribution to a shared outcome.

Grade-Band Strands: Roles & Responsibilities

Each grade band has a distinct role in the shared archive. The strands are interdependent: older students cannot do their work well without the material younger students produce, and younger students' contributions are elevated and given public meaning by what older students do with them.

TK–Grade 2: Raw Material Makers — "My Place"
  • Core Task: Students draw their favorite place in their neighborhood, photograph it (with family coach help), and record a 1–2 minute audio story about why it matters to them.
  • What They Produce: A "Place Card" — an illustrated profile of one local place, with a voice recording attached. These become the primary sources that 6–8 students will map and annotate.
  • Family Coach Role: Active. Coach helps with photography, audio recording setup, and serves as the interview subject for TK students who may need a model. Coach also completes a brief "What I noticed about my child's place" observation form.
  • Driving Question Connection: At this age level, the question becomes: "What is a place that matters to YOU — and why does it matter?"
Grades 3–5: Story Gatherers — "Our Places"
  • Core Task: Students conduct a structured oral history interview with one family member or neighbor about a place that mattered to them (past or present). They write a 3–5 paragraph narrative profile based on the interview.
  • What They Produce: A written narrative with the interview recording as supporting evidence. Narratives are submitted to the shared archive and become source material for 9–12 editors.
  • Oral History Skills Introduced: Preparing questions in advance, listening without interrupting, asking follow-up questions, distinguishing between what the interviewee said and what the student thinks it means.
  • Driving Question Connection: "What does this place mean to the person you interviewed — and what does their answer tell you about the place, or about them?"
Grades 6–8: Mappers & Connectors — "Mapping the Stories"
  • Core Task: Students receive the TK–2 Place Cards and 3–5 oral history narratives and use Google My Maps (or ArcGIS StoryMaps) to create a georeferenced, annotated digital map layer. Each pin on the map is linked to a Place Card or narrative, with a 75–100 word contextual note written by the student.
  • What They Produce: An interactive digital map layer for the archive, organized by county and then by theme (students choose their own thematic categories and must justify them).
  • Critical Thinking Layer: Students must address: "What patterns do you notice across the places submitted? What do those patterns tell you about our school community?"
  • Cross-Grade Feedback Role: Grade 6–8 students provide written feedback to Grade 3–5 narratives before the final submission — one specific observation and one question that the narrative raised for them.
Grades 9–12: Editors & Publishers — "The Archive"
  • Core Task: Students serve as editors and curators of the complete archive. They select 15–20 Place Cards and narratives for inclusion in the published site, write editorial notes for each selection, design the archive's navigation structure and visual identity, and compose an 800–1200 word introduction essay arguing what the archive reveals about Northern California and the Visions community.
  • What They Produce: The published archive itself — a real website built in Google Sites, Cargo, or a comparable platform. The archive goes live at the end of Week 8 and remains accessible to families and the public.
  • Editorial Decision-Making: Students must document their selection decisions in an "Editor's Log" — explaining why they selected or declined each submission, using specific criteria they established as a group.
  • Driving Question at This Level: "What makes a place worth archiving — and what does our collective answer to that question say about who we are?"

Full Lesson Plan: Grade 3–5 Strand, Week 2

The Oral History Interview — Preparation & Fieldwork

Grade 3–5 · Week 2 50 minutes + interview time Async + Fieldwork
Learning Objectives

Students will prepare at least 5 interview questions using the oral history protocol. Students will conduct a structured interview with a family member or community member. Students will make a usable recording of the interview for use in the narrative writing phase.

0–12min
Introduce
What is Oral History? Why Does It Matter?
Student watches a 7-minute teacher-recorded video introducing oral history as a discipline: what it is (capturing lived experience in someone's own words), why it matters (official records often miss the stories of ordinary people), and one famous example (StoryCorps). Video ends with: "You are about to become an oral historian. Your interview will be part of a real archive."
Teacher note: The StoryCorps reference is intentional. Their archive is built on exactly the same premise as this project — ordinary people's stories, recorded and preserved. If time allows, play 90 seconds of an actual StoryCorps recording before the video ends. It makes the purpose viscerally real.
12–30min
Prepare
Write Your Interview Questions
Using the Interview Preparation Guide (see Student Materials), student writes at least 5 interview questions about their subject's place. They start with the required "seed questions" and then write at least 3 of their own. Student also prepares 2–3 follow-up prompts they can use if the conversation stalls: "Can you say more about that?" / "What did it feel like to be there?" / "Why do you think that place mattered so much to you?"

Students practice reading their questions aloud — to themselves, or to a family member who is NOT the interview subject. The goal is fluency, not memorization.
30–50min
Fieldwork
Conduct the Interview
Student conducts the interview with their chosen subject (family member or neighbor). Requirements: the interview must be recorded (phone audio recorder or video call recording); the student must ask all 5 seed questions and at least 2 of their own; the interview should be at least 10 minutes long. After the interview, student writes 3–5 bullet points of "things I heard that I want to include in my narrative."
Teacher note: "At least 10 minutes" sounds arbitrary, but it's not. Shorter interviews tend to stay at the surface. The 10-minute minimum is a gentle forcing function toward depth. Remind students: the most interesting moment in an interview is usually after the first awkward silence, not before it.

Full Lesson Plan: Grade 9–12 Strand, Week 6

Editorial Decisions — Building the Selection Rationale

Grade 9–12 · Week 6 60 minutes Async + Small Group Zoom
Learning Objectives

Students will make and document final selection decisions for the published archive. Students will write editorial notes that contextualize each selected piece for a public audience. Students will negotiate editorial disagreements using evidence-based reasoning.

0–20min
Individual Work
Final Selection Review — Individual Ranking
Working from the shared submission pool, each student independently ranks their top 20 submissions for inclusion. They record their reasoning for each selection and each notable exclusion in the Editor's Log. This individual ranking step happens before the group meeting — the goal is to arrive with a position, not to form one in the meeting.
Teacher note: This sequencing — individual reasoning first, then group deliberation — is borrowed from the Delphi Method used in professional decision-making contexts. Students who form opinions in groups first tend to defer to whoever speaks most confidently. This structure gives quieter students something to stand on.
20–50min
Collaborative
Editorial Board Meeting (Small Group Zoom)
Groups of 3–4 students meet to compare rankings and make collective selection decisions. Teacher facilitates but does not make selections. The group must: agree on the final 15–20 pieces; identify and address geographic gaps (is every county represented?); resolve disagreements by discussing the selection criteria they established in Week 1; and document one piece they disagreed about and how they resolved it.
Teacher note: Disagreements are pedagogically valuable. If a group reaches consensus in 5 minutes without friction, they're probably deferring to each other rather than actually deliberating. You can introduce a gentle challenge: "What's the strongest piece you decided not to include, and why didn't it make the cut?"
50–60min
Write
Draft Editorial Notes
Each student drafts editorial notes for 2–3 of the selected pieces (divided among group members). Each note is 60–80 words: what the piece is, what it contributes to the archive, and why it was selected. Tone: accessible to a general public audience, not academic. These notes appear alongside each piece in the published archive.

Student-Facing Materials

TK–2: Place Card Template — What to Include

Template · TK–2

Your Place Card will be part of the archive for the whole school to see. Here's what to include:

1. A drawing or photograph of your place
Draw it yourself, or take a photo with a grown-up's help. The picture should show what makes the place special — not just what it looks like, but what it feels like.
2. The name of your place
What do you call it? (It doesn't have to be its "official" name. You can call it "the big tree by the park" or "Grandma's garden.")
3. Where it is
What city or town is it in? You don't need to put the street address.
4. Your audio story (1–2 minutes)
Record yourself answering: "Why does this place matter to me?" Tell a story about something that happened there, or explain what you love about it. It's okay to make mistakes — just keep talking.

Grades 3–5: Interview Preparation Guide

Fieldwork Guide · Gr. 3–5

Before your interview, write answers to these setup questions:

Who are you interviewing?
What is their relationship to you?
What place do you think they'll want to talk about? (Make a guess — it may change.)
Why does their story of this place matter to the archive?

Required "Seed Questions" — ask all five:

1. Can you describe a place that really mattered to you growing up — or that matters to you now?

2. What did the place look like? What did it sound like or smell like?

3. What happened there that you still remember?

4. Has the place changed? Is it still there?

5. If you could go back to that place right now, what would you do?

Write 3 of your own questions here:

6. _______________________________________________

7. _______________________________________________

8. _______________________________________________

During the interview, remember:

— Let them finish before asking the next question.
— If something surprises you, it's okay to say "Wait — can you tell me more about that?"
— You don't have to ask every question in order. Follow the conversation.
— If there's a pause, wait. The best answers often come after a pause.

Grades 9–12: Editor's Log Template

Professional Document · Gr. 9–12

Complete one entry per submission you review. This log is a graded assessment artifact — it should show your reasoning, not just your conclusions.

Submission Title / Author:

Grade Band:

County / Location:

Selection Decision: Include · Do Not Include · On the Fence

Reasoning: (2–4 sentences. What does this piece contribute to the archive? What does it do that others don't? If you're not including it — what's missing, or what made something else a stronger choice?)

Questions I'd ask the author if I could: (1–2 questions — things that would help you write a better editorial note, or that the piece left unanswered)

Suggested editorial note draft: (60–80 words for the archive page — if you're including it)

Your final Editor's Log should have entries for every submission you reviewed — selected and declined. The declined entries matter as much as the selected ones. Editorial judgment is visible in what you chose not to include and why.

Grades 6–8: Pattern Analysis Prompt

Writing Task · Gr. 6–8

After completing your map layer and contextual notes, write a 2–3 paragraph analysis responding to this question:

"Looking at all the places submitted to the archive so far: what patterns do you notice? What kinds of places keep appearing? What's absent — what kinds of places seem to be missing? What do these patterns tell you about the Visions student community, or about the nine counties we come from?"

Your analysis should:
— Reference specific place cards or narratives you've read (by title or county, not by student name)
— Identify at least one pattern you found surprising
— Be honest about the limits of your analysis ("This might be because..." or "I can't tell from the map alone whether...")

This analysis becomes the bridge between the map layer and the 9–12 introduction essay. The high school editors will read your analysis as part of their own research for the archive introduction.

Assessment by Grade Band

Each grade band is assessed on their strand's specific products. All grade bands also complete a process reflection at the end of Week 8. The archive itself is a shared outcome — no individual student is scored on its overall quality, only on their specific contribution.

Implementation Notes for Online Context

Coordination Guidance

Managing a TK–12 project across async grade bands

The submission pipeline matters more than any individual lesson. The biggest coordination challenge is ensuring that TK–2 Place Cards and Gr. 3–5 narratives are submitted early enough for 6–8 students to map and annotate them, and early enough for 9–12 students to make selection decisions. Build a one-week buffer into the submission schedule: ask younger grades to submit by end of Week 3, even if the official deadline is Week 4.

The cross-grade feedback in Week 4 is high-leverage. When a high school student writes a genuine response to a third-grader's narrative, and that third-grader sees it, the project becomes real in a way that no rubric can manufacture. Protect this step — don't cut it when time gets tight.

The public exhibition requires advance family communication. Families should know from Day 1 that this project ends with a public archive that will remain online. The launch communication should address: what data is shared (place names and county — no home addresses); how families can opt out of public display while still having their child participate; and how to access the archive after it goes live.

Motivation Design: The Archive Stakes System

Design Rationale

The "upload a photo and two sentences" problem has a specific cause

When students produce minimum-effort work in a PBL project, it's almost never because they're lazy. It's because the stakes don't feel real. A project with a real public audience should solve this — but only if students can actually see what "real" looks like. The Archive Stakes System creates that visibility by making the gap between minimum-effort and genuine-effort submissions obvious, and by making editorial selection a concrete, social reward that students actually want.

The system operates on two levers. First, peer visibility: when students submit a Place Card or narrative, they can see how it compares to other submissions from their county. This is not competitive grading — it's a quality mirror. Students who see strong work from a peer in Redding often revise their own submission from Chico without being asked. Second, editorial selection as social capital: being chosen by the 9–12 editorial team is a concrete, named outcome that students in grades TK–8 genuinely work toward. "I'm going to be in the archive" is a motivational hook that grades cannot replicate.

What this does to the "photo and two sentences" problem: Students who submit minimum-effort work can see immediately that their submission is unlikely to be selected. They aren't penalized — but the gap is visible. Most will revise. Those who don't have made an informed choice, which is different from not caring.

Cross-Grade Motivation Design

The dependency is the engine

The most powerful motivational element in this unit isn't a badge or a score — it's the structural dependency between grade bands. When a 4th-grader knows that a high school student will read their narrative and decide whether it belongs in a public archive, the stakes become real in a way that teacher feedback alone cannot produce. And when a high school editor sees a third-grader's oral history narrative and writes a genuine editorial note about it, both students experience what it means to be part of something larger than a single assignment.

This dependency also addresses the "beautiful idea, uneven execution" critique of PBL: uneven execution is the curriculum. The editorial process is designed to handle variance in submission quality — that's what editors do. The high school students' job is precisely to take raw, uneven material from younger students and make curatorial decisions about it. Their skill is tested by the unevenness, not undermined by it.
