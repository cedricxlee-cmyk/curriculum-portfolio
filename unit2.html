Unit Rationale

Online-Native Design Principle

The irony that becomes the curriculum

Students in online independent study programs are among the highest-exposure users of algorithmic systems: recommendation engines, adaptive learning platforms, content filters, and AI-assisted communication tools are embedded in their daily academic experience. Most have never been asked to think critically about any of it.

This unit treats the student's own algorithmic environment as primary source material. Students don't read about how recommendation engines work — they audit their own feeds over three days and write about what the algorithm apparently thinks they are. They don't study AI bias in the abstract — they analyze real, documented cases and then examine a dataset for bias patterns themselves. The independent study format means students have genuine time for this kind of observational and investigative work that a traditional class period schedule can't accommodate.

The weekly Socratic seminar (60 min, Zoom) functions as the synthesis space. Students arrive having done the investigation; the seminar is for deliberation, not information delivery. This is the model professional ethicists and policy researchers actually use — individual fieldwork feeding collective analysis.

A note on scope: this unit is deliberately not a computer science course. Students do not write code or need a technical background. The focus is on critical reasoning, evidence-based argument, and ethical judgment — skills every high school student needs regardless of career path. Technical concepts are introduced at the level needed to reason about them, not at the level needed to build them.

Essential Questions & Enduring Understandings

Essential Questions
  • How does AI decide what we see, hear, and believe?
  • Who benefits when AI systems are wrong — and who pays the cost?
  • What does it mean to trust a source you can't fully see or verify?
  • Can a machine be biased? Can it be fair?
  • What rights do we have over our own data and attention?
  • What would it look like to design AI that reflects our actual values — and whose values count?
Enduring Understandings
  • Algorithms encode the priorities of whoever built them — neutrality is a myth.
  • AI errors follow patterns; they are not random and do not fall equally on all groups.
  • Media literacy in the 21st century requires understanding computational systems, not just rhetoric.
  • Informed consent in a datafied world requires active knowledge, not passive acceptance of terms of service.
  • Students are already stakeholders in AI governance, whether or not they're treated as such.

Full Lesson Plan: Week 1, Session 1

Unit Launch & Algorithm Audit — Begin 3-Day Field Investigation

Week 1, Session 1 50 minutes Async Self-Directed
Learning Objectives

Students will understand the unit's driving questions and establish a personal stake in the material. Students will begin the Algorithm Audit using the structured observation protocol. Students will start forming a hypothesis about what their algorithmic profile "says" about them.

0–10min
Activate
Opening Provocation: "The Algorithm Knows You"
Student reads a short 2-page excerpt from Cathy O'Neil's Weapons of Math Destruction (teacher-adapted, Lexile-adjusted) describing how algorithmic systems make consequential decisions. No analysis yet — just reading and marginal reaction notes. Prompt: "Mark anything that surprises you, bothers you, or that you want to push back on."
Teacher note: Assign the passage via Google Classroom with comment access turned on. Students who want to discuss before the seminar can post a comment. This is optional but some students will use it.
10–25min
Introduce
Unit Overview + Algorithm Audit Protocol
Student watches a 10-minute teacher-recorded video introducing the six weeks, the essential questions, and the Algorithm Audit assignment. The video explains: what an algorithm is (in plain language), what a "recommendation system" does, and what the audit is asking students to observe. The audit begins today and runs for three days. Students receive the Algorithm Audit Log (see Student Materials).
25–45min
Fieldwork
Day 1 of Algorithm Audit
Student spends 20 minutes on one platform of their choice (YouTube, Spotify, TikTok, Instagram, or a news aggregator app). They use the Algorithm Audit Log to record: what was recommended, what they clicked vs. skipped, what surprised them, and what they think the algorithm "decided" about them based on these recommendations. They note patterns and anomalies. Day 1 entry completed and saved.
Teacher note: Students choose the platform — don't assign one. The point is that they investigate something they actually use. If a student doesn't use social media, news aggregators, YouTube, and music platforms all have recommendation systems that work the same way.
45–50min
Reflect
Initial Hypothesis
Student writes a 2–3 sentence response to: "After one day of observation, what's your first hypothesis about what your algorithm thinks you are? What surprised you most so far?" Posted to the class discussion board — other students can see and react, but substantive responses aren't due until after the 3-day audit is complete.

Full Lesson Plan: Week 3, Session 2 ★

Write the Bias Audit Memo: Finding Patterns in a Dataset

Week 3, Session 2 55 minutes Async Self-Paced
Learning Objectives

Students will identify at least two patterns suggesting bias in a curated dataset. Students will write a professional-genre memo that makes a specific, evidence-based recommendation. Students will distinguish between bias in training data, bias in algorithm design, and bias in deployment context.

0–10min
Context
The Consultant Scenario
Student reads a one-page scenario: they are a junior AI ethics consultant hired by a mid-size technology company to review its hiring algorithm before it goes live. The company has provided a summary dataset of 200 fictional hiring outcomes. The student's job is to identify where the data or outcomes show potential bias, explain what's causing it, and recommend a specific course of action. The memo is due to the executive team in one hour.
Teacher note: The professional framing matters. Students who feel like they're doing a school assignment approach it differently than students who feel like they're advising a real client. The fictional scenario creates stakes. Don't undercut it by framing it as practice.
10–32min
Investigate
Dataset Analysis
Student opens the Hiring Outcomes Dataset (Google Sheets — teacher-curated, fictional data). The dataset includes columns for: name (with implied gender and ethnicity), zip code, previous job title, and hiring outcome (selected / not selected). Students answer guided analysis questions without doing advanced statistics — they look for patterns using the filter and sort functions built into Sheets. Guided questions scaffold the investigation (see Student Materials for the full question set).
Teacher note: The dataset is designed to surface three discoverable patterns: lower selection rates for names coded as female in technical roles, geographic clustering suggesting zip code as a proxy variable, and underrepresentation of candidates without college job titles despite equivalent experience. Students don't need to find all three — finding and clearly explaining one or two is sufficient for the memo.
32–52min
Write
The Audit Memo
Using the Memo Template (see Student Materials), student writes a 1–2 page professional memo structured as: (1) What bias did you find? (2) Who is most affected and how? (3) What caused it — data, design, or deployment? (4) What should the company do? Written to an executive audience: concise, specific, evidence-referenced, action-oriented. No bullet points without explanation. No vague language ("could potentially..."). Every claim should be tied to something they saw in the data.
52–55min
Peer Review
Async Response Post
Student posts their completed memo to the class discussion board. Within 24 hours, they respond to one peer's memo with: one observation about what the memo did well (specific, not generic), and one push question — something the memo claimed that they want to push back on or that they think needs stronger evidence. Push questions must be genuine, not performative.

Full Lesson Plan: Week 6, Session 3 — Panel Defense

Ethical Design Proposal — Live Panel Defense

Week 6, Session 3 75 minutes (full class Zoom) Synchronous · Public
Learning Objectives

Students will present an evidence-based proposal for improving an AI system's ethical dimensions. Students will respond to critical questions from panelists in real time, demonstrating depth of reasoning beyond their prepared materials. Students will practice professional academic discourse in a high-stakes public forum.

0–5min
Setup
Panel Convenes
Teacher opens the Zoom session and introduces the panel format: each student presenter gets 5 minutes to present, followed by 5 minutes of questions from the panel (teacher + 2 student panelists rotating). Audience members may submit written questions via the chat for the teacher to select from. The audience is intentionally included: families and invited guests are welcome to observe.
5–65min
Presentations
Student Presentations (6 students × 10 min each)
Each student presents their Ethical Design Proposal for 5 minutes, then faces 5 minutes of panel questions. The proposal structure: (1) What AI system are you addressing and what ethical problem did you identify? (2) What evidence supports the existence of this problem? (3) What specific design change do you propose? (4) What tradeoffs or objections do you anticipate, and how do you respond to them? Student panelists rotate every two presentations so everyone serves on the panel at least once.
Teacher note: The rotation means that even students who are nervous about presenting are engaged as evaluators before their own turn. This is both a pedagogical and a fairness design — students who watch two presentations before they present are typically more confident and more specific in their own presentations.
65–75min
Close
Closing Reflection + Unit Celebration
Teacher facilitates a brief closing round: each student names one thing they changed their mind about during this unit and one question they're leaving with that they don't yet have an answer to. This is not graded. It's a signal that intellectual humility is the appropriate endpoint of a unit on AI ethics — not certainty.

Student-Facing Materials

Algorithm Audit Log — 3-Day Field Investigation

Investigation Tool

Complete one entry per day for three consecutive days on the same platform. Focus on what you notice, not what you think you should say.

Day _____ · Platform: _____________ · Date: _____________

What was recommended to you today?
List 5–8 specific pieces of content (videos, songs, articles, accounts) that the platform surfaced for you. Note titles or descriptions.
What did you click on? What did you skip?
Mark each item: clicked, skipped, or watched/listened to part of. If you skipped something, note why if you know.
What patterns do you notice?
Are there recurring topics? Specific moods or styles? Things that keep appearing even though you don't remember selecting them? Anything that felt wrong — like the platform misread you?
What do you think the algorithm "decided" about you today?
Based on what it served you, write 2–3 sentences describing the version of you the algorithm appears to believe in. Is that version accurate?

After Day 3: Write a 1-paragraph synthesis: What changed across the three days? Did your hypothesis from Day 1 hold up, change, or get more complicated?

Bias Audit Memo — Template & Dataset Guidance

Performance Task

Dataset Guided Analysis Questions — answer these in your notes before writing the memo:

1. Sort the dataset by "Hiring Outcome." What percentage of candidates were selected overall?

2. Now filter by a demographic variable (try gender-coded names, then zip code). Does the selection rate change? By how much?

3. Find two candidates with similar job titles and experience but different outcomes. What's different about them? What might explain the difference?

4. Are there any groups or zip codes that are completely absent from the "selected" column? What might that tell you?

5. Is the bias you found in the data itself (who applied), in the algorithm's decisions, or is it impossible to tell from this dataset alone? Explain your reasoning.

Memo Template:

TO: Executive Leadership Team
FROM: [Your Name], AI Ethics Review
RE: Preliminary Bias Assessment — Hiring Algorithm v1.0
DATE: [Today's date]

SUMMARY (2–3 sentences: what did you find and what do you recommend?)

FINDINGS (What patterns did you identify? What's the evidence? Who is affected?)

LIKELY CAUSES (Is this a data problem, a design problem, or both? What's your reasoning?)

RECOMMENDATION (What specific action should the company take before launching? Be concrete. "Investigate further" is not a recommendation.)

LIMITATIONS OF THIS ANALYSIS (What can't you determine from this dataset alone? What would you need to know more?)

Length: 1–2 pages. Audience: executive readers who are not AI specialists. Tone: professional, direct, evidence-based. Avoid hedging language unless you have a genuine reason to hedge.

Ethical Design Proposal — Requirements & Evaluation Criteria

Capstone Task

Choose one AI system you interact with regularly (recommendation algorithm, AI writing tool, content moderation system, predictive text, facial recognition, etc.). Identify one ethical problem it has — documented or directly observed — and propose a specific design change to address it.

Written Proposal Requirements (submitted before the panel):

1. System Description — What does it do? Who uses it? Who built it? (1 paragraph)

2. Ethical Problem — What specific harm, bias, or rights violation have you identified? What evidence do you have? (2–3 paragraphs; cite at least two sources)

3. Proposed Design Change — What would you change? Be specific: not "make it more fair" but "remove zip code as a training variable" or "require human review for all denials above a threshold." (1–2 paragraphs)

4. Tradeoffs and Objections — What would your change cost, break, or complicate? Who would push back, and why? How do you respond? (1–2 paragraphs)

5. Reflection — What limits your proposal? What would you need to know more to be more confident? (1 paragraph)

Proposal length: 3–5 pages. Presentation: 5 minutes. Bring questions you expect the panel to ask — preparation is visible.

Motivation Design: The AI Trust Trial

Design Rationale — Why Simulation?

Reflection doesn't change behavior. Experience does.

The gap in most AI ethics curriculum is this: students can write a perfect essay about algorithmic bias and still trust the next AI output they see uncritically. Knowing about a problem is not the same as having felt it. This simulation closes that gap by putting students in situations where their trust instincts are tested against AI outputs they cannot immediately verify — and showing them, in real time, where their calibration breaks down.

The "AI Trust Trial" runs as Week 2's core session. Students interact with a simulated AI assistant across six high-stakes scenarios. After each scenario they make a trust decision — act on the output, verify it, or reject it. Their running Trust Calibration Score updates immediately based on the quality of their decision, not their explanation of it. Students who over-trust see their score drop. Students who under-trust (refuse everything) also see their score drop. The target is calibrated trust — and the score makes that visible in a way no rubric can.

What makes this online-native: This simulation runs in the browser, async, with no teacher present. That's the exact context where AI trust failures actually happen — alone, at a device, with no one to check with. The simulation trains for the real environment.

What the Score Measures
  • Over-trust failures — acting on AI outputs that are confidently wrong, fabricated, or missing crucial context
  • Under-trust failures — rejecting or refusing to act on outputs that are accurate and well-calibrated
  • Verification judgment — knowing when to check a claim vs. when the cost of checking outweighs the risk of being wrong
  • The score reflects decision quality, not stated reasoning — you can't fake it with good writing
Classroom Use Protocol
  • Students complete the trial async, unobserved — this is intentional. The real-world context is unsupervised.
  • Final score and calibration type are submitted as evidence, not self-reported
  • Week 2 seminar opens with: "Where did you over-trust? Where did you under-trust?" — score data makes this specific, not hypothetical
  • Students who score below 60 are flagged for a 1:1 teacher check-in before Week 3

Unit-Wide Gamification Architecture

The simulation is one piece of a broader behavioral scaffolding system. The full gamification layer runs across all six weeks, creating feedback loops that address the core motivation problem: students who disengage quietly in an async environment have nowhere to hide when their decisions are visible and scored.

Design Principle

Behavior visibility, not compliance tracking

The gamification is not about points for completion. It's about making intellectual behavior visible — specifically the behaviors that matter for AI literacy: how carefully you read before trusting, whether you update your position when evidence changes, how you handle genuine uncertainty. Students who are good at looking engaged but not actually thinking get exposed quickly. Students who genuinely grapple — even imperfectly — get credit for it.

What Disengaged Students Can't Fake
  • Trust Calibration Score — produced by behavior, not writing
  • Substantive push questions in peer review — quality is obvious
  • Verification Rate — either you checked or you didn't
  • Panel defense under targeted questioning — preparation is visible in real time
  • Specific pattern observations in audit log — vague observations don't unlock Week 2
What Anxious Students Still Can Access
  • Simulation can be retaken — score improves with reattempt
  • Peer review can be revised before teacher marks it complete
  • Panel prep session in Week 6, Session 1 is low-stakes practice
  • Trust Calibration Score is formative, not summative — it informs, not punishes
  • Students can flag a scenario as "I want to discuss this" before seminar
